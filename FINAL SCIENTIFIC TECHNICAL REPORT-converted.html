1.0 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Overview


The American Centrifuge Demonstration (ACD) Program was a jointly funded, 23-month Research, Development and Demonstration (RD&D) program between the U.S. Department of Energy (80% share) and USEC Inc. (20% share). The RD&D program was run by an integrated team of USEC, B&W and Toshiba domain experts, with oversight by an independent ACD Board of Managers. The seven-person Board was chaired by Luis Reyes, former Executive Director for Operations of the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission, and was comprised of senior executives with a diverse portfolio of experience and expertise in regulation, technology, manufacturing and operations. The program took place from June 2012 through April 2014. The total cost of the program was $352.3 million, of which DOE funded its contractual commitment of $280 million (79.5%) and USEC funded $72.3 million (20.5%) to complete the program.


The first objective of the program was the demonstration of the American Centrifuge technology through the construction and operation of a commercially-staged demonstration cascade of 120 centrifuges. The second objective was to sustain the domestic U.S. centrifuge technical and industrial base for national security purposes and potential commercialization of the American Centrifuge technology, including activities to reduce the risk and improve the future prospects of deployment.


The first objective was successfully achieved through completion of a series of ten Technical Milestones, five Performance Indicators and a series of related tests executed under a formal test program approved by DOE technical experts. The second objective was successfully achieved through a full range of initiatives to mature the project deployment plan over the course of the program. All program milestones, performance indicators and tests were achieved on or ahead of schedule and within the initial budget.


The successful completion of this comprehensive, 23-month RD&D program ensured that the United States has a proven and reliable domestic uranium enrichment technology that is available to support both national and energy security needs. Low enriched uranium is used in nuclear power plants to provide zero carbon-emission electricity to U.S. consumers and to produce tritium which is essential to maintain the U.S. strategic deterrent.

Objective 1 – Technology Demonstration


The first objective of the RD&D program used a DOE-approved Test Program that consisted of jointly developed Technical Milestones, Performance Indicators, and DOE-prescribed Tests to demonstrate the technology’s capabilities in the following four general areas:


  • Cascade SWU1 Production

  • Centrifuge Reliability (under normal operating conditions)

  • System Robustness (under abnormal operating conditions)

  • Centrifuge Manufacturing Quality

The successful demonstration of the above areas provides the technical foundation to deploy a world-class U.S. centrifuge plant that can operate safely and reliably.


A listing of the Technical Milestones and Performance Indicators is included as Appendix A.


Cascade SWU Production

The centerpiece of the RD&D program was the construction, testing and operation of the Commercial Demonstration Cascade (CDC) in Piketon, Ohio. The CDC is a full-scale Commercial Plant cascade with 120 AC100 centrifuges installed in a commercial plant design configuration. The plant design provides for multiple layers of support system redundancy to provide for either continued operation or safe shutdown in the event of a system failure. A cascade is the fundamental building block of a centrifuge uranium enrichment plant. SWU production and operation over a range of product and tails assays needed by commercial nuclear power plants was validated by successful completion of one milestone and related testing that was fully documented and certified as complete by DOE experts.

Milestone 5 - Demonstration of Sustained Production from Cascade Operation


Centrifuge Reliability (under normal operating conditions)

The centrifuge design used in the CDC is designated the “AC100.” This design is the successor of prior DOE designs in the 1980s, which produced about 200 SWU per machine year. Working with Oak Ridge National Laboratory, under a separate, USEC-funded Cooperative Research and Development Agreement (CRADA) signed in 2000, USEC significantly updated

U.S. centrifuge technology via the introduction of state-of-the-art materials, modern electronics, advanced analytical tools, and automated manufacturing techniques. Today, the AC100 is the most productive centrifuge in the world, with a demonstrated production level several multiples of any foreign enricher’s technology. Reliable operation of the AC100 under normal operating conditions was validated by the successful completion of two Milestones, one Performance Indicator and other testing fully documented and certified as complete by DOE experts.

System Robustness (under abnormal operating conditions)

The centrifuge plant has a planned design life of 30 years. During that period, it is reasonable to expect that the AC100 centrifuge may be exposed to off–normal operating conditions due to unplanned loss of support systems that affect normal operation. While support systems are designed and tested to be reliable, temporary losses may occur. Systems should be sufficiently robust to withstand temporary losses without damage to centrifuges or other systems. The robustness of the AC100 centrifuge, the demonstration cascade, and support systems under abnormal operating conditions were validated by the successful completion of three Milestones, two performance indicators and other testing that was fully documented and certified as complete by DOE experts.

Centrifuge Manufacturing Quality

Since 2002, USEC has worked to reconstitute the U.S. centrifuge manufacturing infrastructure that was demobilized more than 20 years ago when DOE terminated the previous

U.S. centrifuge program in the mid-1980s. In 2011, USEC and Babcock & Wilcox (B&W) jointly launched American Centrifuge Manufacturing, LLC (ACM) to provide integrated manufacturing and assembly of centrifuge machines for the American Centrifuge Plant in Piketon, Ohio. ACM is principally located in a specially designed facility in Oak Ridge, Tennessee and draws on the expertise of hundreds of suppliers across the United States for parts and material for AC100 centrifuges. The ability of ACM to build AC100 centrifuges with consistent high quality is necessary to ensure that the other major areas of SWU production, centrifuge reliability and system robustness can be achieved. The AC100 manufacturing processes were validated by the successful completion of one Milestone and two Performance Indicators that were fully documented and were certified as complete by DOE experts.

Conclusion

As evidenced by the successful completion of the RD&D program, the American Centrifuge technology has attained a high level of technical maturity. The independent ACD Board of Managers, which monitored program execution throughout the RD&D program, concluded that the American Centrifuge technology is currently ready for deployment when the remaining industrial plant systems are added. This level of maturity equates to Technology Readiness Level 8, per the widely accepted government standard (Appendix B). The completion of all the Technical Milestones, Performance Indicators, and DOE Tests was independently verified by DOE program management after review and consultation with their technical team. In addition to demonstrating the commercial readiness of the American Centrifuge technology, it is noteworthy that the RD&D program also met a comprehensive set of prerequisites outlined by the DOE prior to testing. The RD&D Program Technical Milestones, Performance Indicators and DOE Tests were also completed either on or ahead of schedule and within the initial budget. The American Centrifuge is technically ready for deployment to serve both the national security and energy needs of the United States. Issues and opportunities for improvement identified during testing are being pursued after conclusion of the RD&D program.

2.0 OBJECTIVES OF THE PROGRAM


The American Centrifuge RD&D program had two objectives: to demonstrate the American Centrifuge technology through the construction and operation of one or more demonstration cascades of 120 AC100 centrifuges and to sustain the domestic U.S. centrifuge technical and industrial base for national security purposes and potential commercialization of the American Centrifuge technology, including activities to reduce the risk and improve the future prospects of deployment of the American Centrifuge.


The first objective of the RD&D program was technology demonstration. This was accomplished via a jointly developed Test Program that included Technical Milestones, Performance Indicators, and DOE Tests. Agreement on the Test Program itself was Technical Milestone 1, which provided:


DOE and USEC jointly agree upon a test program for the remaining Milestones and for full system reliability and plant availability that takes into account human factors, upgraded Lower Suspension Drive Assembly (LSDA) and overall AC100 reliability, and full cascade separative performance, so as to achieve overall plant availability objectives.

Technical Milestone 1 was completed by June 30, 2012. There were three revisions to the Test Program as the program progressed, and program plans were successfully modified to respond to Technical Milestones 1.1 and 1.5. Technical Milestone 1.1 provided:


DOE and USEC will jointly agree to revise and further define the DOE Test Program Plan in Attachment 1 to the RD&D Test Program to include agreed upon parameters and success criteria for Tests and such other modifications as the parties agree, such as the inclusion of additional milestones.

Technical Milestone 1.5 provided:


DOE and USEC will jointly agree to revise and further define the DOE Test Program in Attachment 1 to the RD&D Test Program to include agreed upon parameters and success criteria to test a range of demonstration cascade operational parameters and configurations.

Prior to DOE Tests, a comprehensive set of testing prerequisites outlined by DOE were satisfied.


Technical Milestones represent major programmatic or technical achievements related to demonstration of productive, reliable and high-quality centrifuge operation. Performance Indicators represent either “way points” towards completion of a Technical Milestone or represent ancillary technical issues related to robust centrifuge operation. DOE Tests represent additional areas that DOE required to be investigated to provide added information to demonstrate that the American Centrifuge technology is adequate for national security purposes.

The successful accomplishment of the Test Program demonstrated the American Centrifuge technology’s capabilities in the following four major areas:


  • Cascade SWU Production

  • Centrifuge Reliability (under normal operating conditions)

  • System Robustness (under abnormal operating conditions)

  • Centrifuge Manufacturing Quality

The specific objectives of the RD&D program are delineated in the Test Program as follows:

  1. Show plant availability at target levels

  2. Operate the Balance-of-Plant (BOP) systems in a plant-prototypic arrangement

  3. Demonstrate quality manufacturing and production capability of AC100 centrifuges


  4. Demonstrate robustness of AC100 centrifuge and BOP systems

  5. Provide lessons learned which could assist in future deployment of the technology

  6. Produce evidentiary documentation to verify that Program Technical Milestones, Performance Indicators and DOE Tests have been achieved


The second objective of the program was to sustain the domestic U.S. centrifuge technical and industrial base for national security purposes and potential commercialization of the American Centrifuge technology, including activities to reduce the risk and improve the future prospects of deployment of the American Centrifuge.


The program’s scope of work called for USEC to sustain the technical and industrial base to support future deployment through specific activities beyond accomplishment of the Milestones, Performance Indicators and Tests associated with Objective #1. The work scope included activities such as centrifuge SWU performance and value engineering work, continued manufacture of AC100 centrifuges beyond the 120 centrifuges required for the CDC, improvement in centrifuge manufacturing processes, plant design advancement, design and fabrication of machine electronic packages, limited rate fabrication of specialty equipment for feed and withdrawal, and evaluations of alternative deployment scenarios.

As summarized in the next section, the above objectives were all met during the RD&D program. Completion of these objectives ensures that, from a technology perspective, the United States has a proven, reliable domestic source of uranium enrichment technology available to support both national security and energy needs.

3.0 SUMMARY OF PROGRAM ACCOMPLISHMENTS


Objective 1 – Technology Demonstration


  1. DEMONSTRATION OF CASCADE SWU PRODUCTION

    The centerpiece of the RD&D program was the construction, testing and operation of the Commercial Demonstration Cascade (CDC). Centrifuges in a cascade are arranged in a multi-stage configuration that is capable of producing the assay range required for commercial nuclear power reactors. The CDC is a full-scale cascade with 120 identical AC100 centrifuges. The AC100 centrifuge has an average performance that exceeds 340 SWU per year. The CDC can efficiently produce commercial product assays for commercial reactors.



    The CDC is the “basic building block” of a U.S. centrifuge plant. Six cascades, with 720 AC100 centrifuges, are called a “train”. The two existing Process Buildings in Piketon (see photo below) are designed to accommodate up to 16 trains (96 cascades).


    Using an average centrifuge performance of 340 SWU per year, each train has a capacity of more than 245,000 SWU per year, and a plant with 16 trains has a capacity of more than 3,700,000 SWU per year. These levels of production can provide the enriched uranium required for U.S. national security purposes and dozens of commercial nuclear power reactors in the United States and around the world.


    Technical Milestone 5 was designed to demonstrate that the CDC provides direct evidence of the ability of a train or plant to meet required SWU production and assay levels.


    Technical Milestone 5 - Demonstration of Sustained Production from Cascade Operation

    The completion of Milestone 5 demonstrated that the CDC can operate at design conditions and produce enough separative work to meet or exceed the expected production for the period of operation. The test was conducted in accordance with the NRC license for the American Centrifuge Demonstration Facility, which allows the cascade to operate in “recycle mode.” In this mode of operation, the enriched product from the top stage of the cascade is combined with the depleted tails from the bottom stage and re-fed (i.e., recycled) back into the feed stage so that no enriched product is collected. This “closed-loop” configuration makes the CDC different than an enrichment plant, where the enriched product is collected for sale and use in nuclear reactors. In spite of this difference, the CDC demonstrated the SWU productivity, that is, the capacity to enrich uranium, that was expected. Milestone 5 provided:


    Demonstrate sustained production from commercially staged, 120-centrifuge demonstration cascade configuration for 60 days (~20 machine-years) in cascade recycle mode with expected production availability using an average AC100 centrifuge production of 340 SWU per centrifuge-year.

In accordance with the Test Program, Technical Milestone 5 was successfully completed by achieving the required SWU production by December 31, 2013. This result was reported to and accepted by DOE.


  1. DEMONSTRATION OF CENTRIFUGE RELIABILITY

    (Under normal operating conditions)

    The AC100 centrifuge is the most advanced and productive centrifuge in the world. The AC100 has demonstrated a production level that exceeds 340 SWU per year. The reliability of the AC100 is underpinned by the previous DOE design. The figure below shows the five major subassemblies that make up the AC100; namely, (1) the upper suspension, (2) the rotor, (3) the column, (4) the casing, and (5) the lower suspension drive. In addition, a set of electronic equipment (Machine Instrumentation Package, Motor Drive Unit and Power Quality Filter) is associated with each centrifuge as shown below. The overall reliability of the AC100 is a result of the reliability of the individual subassemblies and electronic equipment associated with each centrifuge.


    Technical Milestones 2 and 4, Performance Indicator A and other testing served to demonstrate that the AC100 centrifuge and its associated electronic equipment is highly reliable under normal operating conditions in the CDC.


    Technical Milestone 2 - Confirmation of LSDA Reliability

    The completion of Technical Milestone 2 demonstrated the adequacy of a design change to the Lower Suspension Drive Assembly (LSDA). Previous operation of AC100 centrifuges in 2010 successfully demonstrated the reliability of all subassemblies with one exception - the LSDA. As a result, the LSDA design was modified, and AC100 centrifuges with the upgraded LSDA design were installed in the CDC. Technical Milestone 2 provided:


    Confirm the reliability of the Lower Suspension Drive Assembly (LSDA) by accumulating 20 machine-years of operation at target speed using AC100 centrifuges with upgraded LSDAs.

In accordance with the Test Program, Technical Milestone 2 was completed by June 1, 2012. AC100 centrifuges with the upgraded LSDAs operated for 20 machine-years without any failures. This result was reported to and accepted by DOE.

Technical Milestone 4 - Demonstration of AC100 Reliability

The completion of Technical Milestone 4 demonstrated the reliability of the AC100 centrifuge with the safety modification and the upgraded LSDA design under design operating conditions in the CDC. The startup and operation of 120 AC100 centrifuges achieved 20 machine-years of operation. This period of operation provided the objective confidence in reliability. Technical Milestone 4 provided:


Demonstrate AC100 reliability by accumulating 20 machine-years with AC100 design with a safety modification at target speed and design condition.

In accordance with the Test Program, Technical Milestone 4 was completed by December 31, 2013. Testing of the machines resulted in failure rates well below expected values for the period of operation. AC100 centrifuges have been demonstrated to meet reliability expectations after 20 machine-years of operation at design conditions to at least the objective confidence level. This result was reported to and accepted by DOE.


Performance Indicator A - Demonstration of AC100 Operational Readiness

After an event in 2011 that resulted in a breach of one centrifuge casing, the AC100 design was modified to include a safety feature that precludes the possibility of recurrence of this failure mode. The completion of Performance Indicator A demonstrated that this added design feature has no adverse performance characteristic during AC100 centrifuge operations.

Performance Indicator A provided:


Demonstrate ACl00 operational readiness by accumulating 10 machine-years with a safety modification at target speed and on gas.

In accordance with the Test Program, Performance Indicator A was completed by October 1, 2012. The accumulation of 10 machine-years of on-gas operation corresponds to an indicative confidence level in the reliability forecast and showed good progress towards demonstrating at least the objective level confidence in AC100 reliability required by Milestone 4 (discussed below) in the Test Program. Thus, the successful completion of Performance Indicator A provided interim assurance (a “way point”) that relevant manufacturing and operations personnel, systems and processes had been properly established for the AC100 with a safety modification. This result was reported to and accepted by DOE.


Post-Program Observation on Centrifuge Reliability

In February 2014, following successful completion of the 60-day commercial demonstration cascade operation test at the end of 2013, the commercial demonstration cascade was discovered to be experiencing an issue that is likely common to all AC100 centrifuges in the cascade. While details are classified, failure to resolve this issue could increase maintenance costs over the life of a centrifuge plant that could impact commercial plant economics. As with other matters that ACD has addressed throughout the American Centrifuge technology

development program, mitigating actions are being evaluated and implemented, and are expected to successfully resolve the issue.

  1. DEMONSTRATION OF CENTRIFUGE AND SYSTEM ROBUSTNESS

    (Under abnormal operating conditions)

    The Balance-of-Plant (BOP) support systems configuration and performance must be able to provide the appropriate operational requirements for both normal and off-normal conditions.

    The AC100 centrifuges could be exposed to off–normal operations during their 30-year life due to excursions of the BOP support systems such as loss of electrical power. Technical Milestones 1.2, 1.3 and 1.4, Performance Indicators D and E, and other DOE Testing served to demonstrate that the AC100 centrifuge and BOP support systems are sufficiently robust under abnormal operating conditions to maintain both safe operation and high production availability.


    Technical Milestone 1.2 - Extended Feed Rate Range Survey, Machine

    The completion of Technical Milestone 1.2 demonstrated that an individual AC100 centrifuge will survive feed rate excursions. The key test objective for Milestone 1.2 provided:


    Demonstrate that an individual machine can survive feed rate excursions (overfeeding and underfeeding) without adverse effects.

In accordance with the Test Program, Technical Milestone 1.2 was successfully completed by June 15, 2013. The relevant data for this test was submitted to and accepted by DOE.


Technical Milestone 1.3 - Machine Performance Parameter Test

The completion of Technical Milestone 1.3 demonstrated that rigorous testing was performed to verify operational parameters during a motor drive power outage with full gas inventory. The performance of this Milestone was part of the initial design verification process used to ensure the AC100 design can survive an external loss of power without damage. The key test objective for Technical Milestone 1.3 provided:


Verify the operational parameters during a motor drive power outage with full gas inventory.

In accordance with the Test Program, Technical Milestone 1.3 was completed by June 30, 2013. The design verification testing to verify AC100 operational parameters was performed in K-1600 Building in Oak Ridge in specially instrumented test stands (see photo).

The centrifuge response during a motor drive power outage was observed, measured and documented. This test was fulfilled by providing DOE with completed test documentation for their evaluation. DOE assessed that the tests provided were satisfactory and met the objective of Milestone

1.3. The relevant data was submitted to and accepted by DOE.


Technical Milestone 1.4 - Power Outage Testing, Machine Response


The completion of Technical Milestone 1.4 demonstrated that AC100 centrifuges and BOP plant support systems can survive an external loss of power event. Prior to the RD&D program, a loss of power event was experienced in a smaller demonstration test cascade (Lead Cascade) in Piketon in June 2011, which did not have the system redundancies and support systems planned for the commercial plant. A number of centrifuge machines were damaged during the event. The key test objective for DOE Test 5 provided:


Demonstrate that machines and support equipment can withstand a temporary, complete loss of externally supplied power to the motor drive without sustaining damage.

In accordance with the Test Program, Technical Milestone 1.4 was completed by August 31, 2013. Technical Milestone 1.4 secured electric power to a test group of AC100 centrifuges that were operating at target speed with no gas. The centrifuges coasted to rest and were successfully restored back to target speed. The evaluation of the test data showed that the centrifuges and support systems met the acceptance criteria. Details of the data, evaluation and conclusions have been documented and the relevant data was submitted to and accepted by DOE.

Performance Indicator D - Demonstration of Robustness of Plant Support Systems

As part of the RD&D program, the BOP support systems were expanded to provide commercial plant-level backup systems. This expansion provided redundancies in the CDC support systems based on the latest System Requirement Documents for the commercial plant. The completion of Performance Indicator D demonstrated that these added redundancies provide robustness (continued cascade operation or safe shutdown capability) under abnormal operating conditions. Performance Indicator D provided:


Demonstrate robustness of plant support systems by successfully completing Integrated Systems Test Program to fully test plant support systems backup and redundant capability to ensure continued cascade operation or safe shutdown of cascade operation in the event of loss of normal power or other system casualties.

In accordance with the Test Program, Performance Indicator D was completed by June 30, 2013. Following the construction of the BOP expansions, Integrated System Test Plans (ISTPs) were performed that assured proper installation and functionality. These ISTPs validated the operation of individual systems, as well as the integrated operations, relative to system backup and redundant capability.


Performance Indicator D was completed when the last ISTP related to verification of plant support systems backup and redundant capability was completed. A directory, along with the completed ISTPs with additionally requested tests, diagrams and data, was reported to and accepted by DOE.


Performance Indicator E - Demonstration of Robustness of AC100 Design

The successful completion of Performance Indicator E demonstrated the degree to which the AC100 centrifuge can successfully survive reasonably anticipated off-normal environments. Performance Indicator E provided:


Demonstrate robustness of AC100 design to stress transients through validated analytical models, simulation and drills (e.g. physical tests) that demonstrate the ability of the cascade to withstand loss of power or other loss of Balance of Plant support system scenarios using a technical evaluation of time and actions to restore support systems and its effect on the AC100 centrifuge starting from steady-state operations.

In accordance with the Test Program, Performance Indicator E was completed by October 1, 2013. Even with the demonstrated robustness of expanded BOP support systems (see Performance Indicator D above), there remains the potential for temporary interruption in plant services to the AC100 centrifuge. Consequently, models, tests and results were used to evaluate the behavior of the AC100 during BOP scenarios identified and developed by the Integrated Product Teams (IPTs) and subject matter experts, with input from Operations personnel in Piketon. The evaluation of models, simulations and test results that demonstrated the robustness of the AC100 when BOP service is temporarily unavailable were documented, submitted to and accepted by DOE.

These components were shipped to ACP in Piketon, Ohio, assembled by ACM and certified for release for operations. Demonstrate AC100 manufacturing quality by operating the Commercial Demonstration Cascade at an objective confidence level for a minimum of 20 machine-years.

4.0 Impact of the Program Results

The successful completion of the RD&D program provides the United States with an advanced domestic centrifuge uranium enrichment technology that has been thoroughly demonstrated to be productive, reliable, robust, and of high quality. As a wholly domestic invention and advancement, the American Centrifuge technology can support both national security and energy needs.


With the ceasing of enrichment at the 60-year-old Paducah Gaseous Diffusion Plant in 2013, the United States currently has no domestic facility that uses U.S.-origin technology to enrich uranium. The Paducah plant was the last operating plant of the three gaseous diffusion plants built by the United States government. International treaty obligations prohibit the United States from using, for military purposes, foreign-produced enriched uranium or uranium enriched in this country using foreign-source technology. Thus, the success of the RD&D program provides a demonstrated technology that can be deployed to address the inevitable shortage of strategic nuclear materials as current enriched uranium stockpiles are used and exhausted.


The United States has a diversified energy portfolio with respect to electrical energy generation from nuclear power. Nearly 20% of electrical power is generated from 100 nuclear power plants in 31 states. In stark contrast to this diversity, about 90% of the world’s nuclear enrichment in 2012 came from only 4 producers in 6 countries – AREVA in France, TENEX in Russia, URENCO in Germany, Holland and the United Kingdom, and USEC in the United States. Two of the aforementioned suppliers, AREVA and URENCO, are tightly linked by the Enrichment Technology Company joint venture that develops and manufactures centrifuges for both companies and by cooperative business arrangements. With the closure of the Paducah plant discussed above, the U.S. fleet of nuclear power plants will find themselves with fewer choices and could become wholly dependent on foreign-owned sources of enrichment in the future. The success of the RD&D program provides a demonstrated technology that can be deployed commercially to provide diversity and price competition in the enrichment marketplace. Experts consider that without an American Centrifuge commercial plant, enrichment buyers are at greater risk for higher enrichment prices and less attractive contract terms.

In summary, the results of the RD&D program provide a technically ready option for the United States to continue to enrich uranium for national security and energy purposes.

Appendix B - Technical Readiness Assessment

Technology development is defined as the process of demonstrating new or unproven technology, the application of existing technology to new or different uses, or the combination of existing and proven technology to achieve a specific goal. In the case of the American Centrifuge technology, USEC and DOE jointly developed a Test Program to demonstrate high confidence in productivity, reliability and robustness to enable a decision whether to deploy a uranium enrichment plant using advanced U.S. centrifuge technology.


The independent ACD Board of Managers endorsed the technical readiness of the American Centrifuge technology stating the following:


“The Board is confident that the American Centrifuge is ready for deployment when included in a uranium enrichment facility that incorporates feed and withdrawal capability as part of a plant deployment”

The successful reduction in technical risk was acknowledged by DOE.


To assist in the determination of Technology Readiness Assessments (TRAs), DOE has developed and issued a technology readiness assessment guide. This document uses a proven NASA and Department of Defense technology assessment model that identifies those elements and processes of technology development required to reach maturity levels that ensure project success. A successful project is a project that satisfies its intended purpose in a safe, timely, and cost-effective manner that is defensible to expert reviewers.


A Technology Readiness Level (TRL) scale used to conduct Technology Readiness Assessments is provided below. The TRL indicates the maturity of a given technology, as defined in Table below. The TRL scale ranges from 1 (basic principle observed) through 9 (total system used successfully in project operations).


Subject to DOE’s independent evaluation, the Technology Readiness of the American Centrifuge technology comports to Level 8, called System Commissioning. This signifies that the “technology has been proven to work in its final form and under expected conditions.” There is confidence that the American Centrifuge can attain the highest Technology Readiness Level of 9 once uranium feed and withdrawal capability are added as part of a plant deployment.
